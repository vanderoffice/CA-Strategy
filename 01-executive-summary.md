# California Enterprise Modernization Plan
## Executive Summary

**Prepared for:** Governor's Office, Office of Data and Innovation, Government Operations Agency
**Date:** December 8, 2025
**Version:** Executive Draft v1.2

---

## The Problem

California state government serves 40 million residents through approximately 170 departments, yet it lacks the natural forcing mechanism that drives private sector efficiency. Without market pressure or competition, government agencies accumulate technical debt, maintain outdated systems, and perpetuate inefficient processes that frustrate both employees and the public.

The consequences are significant:

- **Legacy systems consume 70-80% of IT budgets** — Money that could fund new services instead maintains decades-old technology
- **Procurement cycles average 12-18 months** — By the time solutions are purchased, requirements have changed
- **Data sharing agreements take 12-18 months to negotiate** — Agencies cannot collaborate effectively to serve residents
- **400+ independent systems fragment citizen data** — Residents must re-enter information for every interaction with government
- **33% of government workers have low or no digital skills** — Workforce capability hasn't kept pace with technology

This plan addresses these systemic challenges through coordinated organizational, technical, and cultural change.

---

## The AI Imperative

Artificial intelligence is transforming how organizations deliver services, and government cannot afford to fall behind. AI offers unprecedented opportunities to reduce wait times, automate routine paperwork, personalize citizen interactions, and free employees to focus on complex cases that require human judgment. But these benefits only materialize for organizations with the foundational capabilities to adopt AI responsibly.

California state government currently lacks these foundations:

- **Fragmented data** prevents AI systems from accessing the comprehensive information they need to be useful—an AI assistant cannot help a resident navigate benefits if each program's data lives in an isolated system
- **Outdated infrastructure** cannot support modern AI tools and platforms, leaving departments unable to deploy solutions that are standard in the private sector
- **Rigid procurement** cannot keep pace with rapidly evolving AI capabilities—by the time a contract is signed, the technology has advanced
- **Workforce skill gaps** leave employees unable to effectively oversee and collaborate with AI systems, creating risk of misuse or missed opportunities
- **Absent governance frameworks** create risk of inconsistent, inequitable, or unreliable AI deployment across departments

The E3 Operational Model addresses each of these barriers. Data governance creates the unified, high-quality data AI requires. Modern procurement enables rapid acquisition of AI capabilities as they mature. The talent pipeline builds workforce readiness for human-AI collaboration. And the Independent Office can establish consistent standards for responsible AI use across all departments.

Without this transformation, California will watch the gap widen between what residents experience from private sector services and what government can deliver. With it, California can lead in demonstrating how AI makes government more efficient for taxpayers and more responsive for the residents it serves.

## The Solution: The E3 Operational Model

The **Enterprise Efficiency and Effectiveness (E3) Operational Model** creates institutional accountability for modernization through three integrated components that work together as a system. No single component succeeds in isolation—leadership without modern procurement remains bottlenecked, procurement reform without data governance cannot enable cross-agency services, and data governance without empowered leadership lacks enforcement.

**1. Institutional Accountability** — New leadership positions with actual authority:
- *Undersecretaries for Efficiency and Effectiveness* at Agency level (8-12 positions) — Strategic leaders who coordinate across agency and department boundaries, with final approval authority on technology investments over $5M
- *Chief Deputy Directors for Efficiency and Effectiveness* at Department level (30-40 positions) — Operational leaders embedded in departments who own specific modernization initiatives and performance outcomes
- *Transformation Management Office (TMO)* within Government Operations Agency (GovOps) — Central coordination hub (16-23 staff) managing the statewide portfolio, tracking performance, and resolving cross-agency challenges

**2. Product-Led Delivery** — Modern approaches replacing traditional waterfall development:
- Service mesh architecture connecting systems via Application Programming Interfaces (APIs), enabling systems to share data without costly point-to-point integrations
- $15M modular contracting cap forcing vendors to deliver working software in smaller increments, reducing risk and enabling course correction
- Request for Innovative Ideas (RFI²) procurement that defines problems rather than prescribing solutions, allowing vendors to propose innovative approaches validated through paid prototypes

**3. Data Governance Foundation** — Cross-branch authority enabling collaboration:
- Independent Office of State Data Governance with 9-member board representing legislative, executive, and judicial branches, ensuring durability across political transitions
- Standardized data sharing agreement templates reducing negotiation time from 12-18 months to 4-8 weeks for common scenarios
- Binding statewide data standards that all agencies must follow, creating consistency across the enterprise

See [Governance Model](02-governance-model.md) for complete details on the E3 structure, leadership roles, and the Independent Office.

## Talent Pipeline

Organizational change requires people with new skills. The talent strategy addresses both immediate leadership needs and long-term workforce capacity:

**Governor's Innovation Fellowship** — A 6-month intensive program producing 60 trained modernization leaders annually across three cohorts. Fellows complete a foundational bootcamp covering data governance, agile delivery, and change management, then embed in departments to lead real projects. After graduation, Fellows join the Senior Fellow network, providing ongoing advisory capacity and creating a growing community of modernization expertise across state government.

**Permanent Data Governance Teams** — 438-530 new positions statewide (30 dedicated department teams plus 6-8 regional hubs) provide sustainable, permanent capacity for data management, analysis, and cross-agency coordination. These teams ensure modernization isn't dependent on temporary initiatives but becomes embedded in how government operates.

**Workforce Development** — CalAcademy curriculum tracks provide continuous upskilling for existing employees, with specialized training aligned to the new competencies required by the E3 model.

See [Talent Development](03-talent-development.md) for Fellowship curriculum, team structures, and labor relations strategy.

## Funding

Modernization requires dedicated funding isolated from annual budget pressures. The **California Breakthrough Modernization Fund** ($100M: 50% state, 50% private sector) operates on portfolio management principles similar to venture capital:

- **Quick Wins (40%)** — Small projects ($500K-$2M) with 6-month timelines that demonstrate value and build momentum
- **Strategic Initiatives (40%)** — Medium projects ($2M-$10M) addressing significant operational improvements
- **Transformational (20%)** — Larger investments ($10M-$15M) tackling systemic challenges with higher risk but greater potential impact

The fund includes a built-in sustainability mechanism: 25% of realized savings from successful projects flow back into the fund, creating self-sustaining modernization capacity. By Year 3, the fund is projected to be 30-50% self-sustaining.

See [Funding & Implementation](04-funding-implementation.md) for investment criteria, governance, and sustainability calculations.

## Procurement Innovation

Traditional government procurement is a major barrier to modernization. Lengthy cycles, rigid requirements, and large monolithic contracts result in outdated solutions and vendor lock-in. The plan transforms procurement from bottleneck to enabler:

**Enterprise Vendor Pools** — Pre-qualified vendors organized by capability area (Agile Digital Services, Data & Analytics, Cloud & Platform, Cybersecurity, Change Management) enable task orders in days rather than months. Departments select from vetted vendors without repeating full procurement processes.

**Modular Contracting** — A $15M cap on individual contracts forces work into manageable increments. Smaller contracts mean faster delivery, easier oversight, reduced risk, and the ability to change vendors if performance lags.

**Rapid RFI² (Request for Innovative Ideas)** — Challenge-based procurement that describes problems rather than prescribing solutions. Vendors submit concept papers, and promising approaches receive paid prototype funding ($50K-$150K) to validate feasibility before full investment. The entire process completes in 8-10 weeks.

**Delegated Ordering Authority** — E3 leaders receive authority to issue task orders against vendor pools without navigating traditional approval chains, enabling rapid response to emerging needs.

See [Procurement Guide](05-procurement-guide.md) for complete procurement frameworks, templates, and small business considerations.

## How the Plan Components Interrelate

This plan is designed as an integrated system where each component reinforces the others. Understanding these connections is essential for successful implementation:

**Governance Enables Everything Else**

The E3 leadership structure (Undersecretaries, Chief Deputy Directors, TMO) provides the organizational backbone for all other initiatives. Without leaders who have actual authority and accountability, procurement reforms remain policy documents, data governance lacks enforcement, and modernization projects lack executive sponsorship. The [Governance Model](02-governance-model.md) must be established first to create the institutional capacity for change.

**Talent Sustains the Model**

E3 leaders need skilled teams to execute their mandates. The Governor's Innovation Fellowship creates an immediate pipeline of trained modernization leaders who understand both the technical and organizational dimensions of change. The permanent Data Governance Teams provide ongoing capacity so modernization doesn't depend on temporary initiatives. Without the talent strategy in [Talent Development](03-talent-development.md), the E3 structure would be leadership without troops.

**Funding Fuels Execution**

Even empowered leaders with skilled teams cannot modernize without resources. The Breakthrough Fund in [Funding & Implementation](04-funding-implementation.md) provides dedicated capital isolated from annual budget politics. The fund's portfolio approach—allocating investments across quick wins, strategic initiatives, and transformational projects—mirrors the phased implementation timeline, ensuring resources align with organizational readiness.

**Procurement Translates Strategy to Action**

The best-funded initiatives fail if procurement takes 18 months and locks agencies into decade-long contracts. The procurement innovations in [Procurement Guide](05-procurement-guide.md) give E3 leaders the tools to move quickly: pre-qualified vendor pools, modular contracts, and delegated ordering authority. Rapid RFI² enables the innovative solutions that distinguish modernization from mere system replacement.

**Data Governance Enables Cross-Agency Services**

Citizens experience government as a fragmented maze because agencies cannot share data effectively. The Independent Office of State Data Governance creates binding standards and streamlined agreements that enable the cross-agency collaboration required for citizen-centric services. Without data governance, each modernization project remains an island.

**Readiness Assessment Sequences Deployment**

Not all departments are equally prepared for transformation. The [Readiness Assessment Framework](readiness-assessment/README.md) evaluates departments across six domains to identify where E3 deployment will succeed and where foundational work is needed first. This evidence-based approach prevents wasted investment and builds momentum through demonstrated successes.

**The Virtuous Cycle**

When functioning together, these components create a reinforcing cycle: E3 leaders use Breakthrough Fund resources to launch projects via streamlined procurement, staffed by Fellowship graduates and Data Governance Teams, following standards set by the Independent Office. Successful projects generate savings that replenish the fund, demonstrate value that builds political support, and create case studies that ease adoption in less-ready departments. Each success makes the next one easier.
