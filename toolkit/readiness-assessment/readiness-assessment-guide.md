# Part 1: Strategic Context

> **Section Summary:** This section explains why California needs a standardized readiness assessment, how it aligns with state technology goals, and what benefits it delivers.

## Purpose and Context

California faces a critical challenge in modernizing the technology infrastructure across its 170 state government departments. With limited modernization funding available through the California Department of Technology's (CDT) Technology Modernization Fund and state budget allocations, it is essential to identify and prioritize departments that possess the organizational readiness to immediately begin modernization work and deliver successful outcomes.

This assessment framework provides a standardized, objective methodology to evaluate organizational readiness across all California state departments. By measuring key dimensions of technological maturity, institutional capacity, and execution readiness, this assessment enables data-driven allocation of modernization funding to departments most likely to succeed and deliver tangible value to California residents.

## Strategic Importance

The State of California has made significant investments in digital transformation through initiatives such as **Envision 2026**, the **State Digital Strategy**, and the **Technology Modernization Fund**. However, not all departments are equally prepared to execute complex modernization projects. Past experience has demonstrated that organizational readiness—including leadership commitment, technical infrastructure, workforce capability, and project management maturity—is as critical to project success as the availability of funding.

### Alignment with California's Technology Goals

This assessment directly supports California's strategic technology objectives:

**Advancing an Inclusive Digital Experience**
- Prioritizes departments that can deliver user-centered digital services
- Ensures technology investments improve accessibility and equity for all Californians
- Focuses resources on departments committed to inclusive service design

**Securing Technology Investments**
- Identifies departments with mature cybersecurity and risk management practices
- Reduces risk of security incidents and data breaches
- Ensures compliance with state security mandates (SAM/SIMM 5300)

**Strengthening the Technology Workforce**
- Recognizes departments with modern skills and change-ready cultures
- Supports workforce development through capability assessment
- Identifies training and capacity-building opportunities

**Aligning Strategy and Execution**
- Ensures funding flows to departments with governance structures that support modernization
- Connects technology investments to strategic plans and measurable outcomes
- Promotes transparency and accountability in technology decision-making

## Assessment Framework Overview

The assessment evaluates **six critical domains** of organizational readiness, each containing specific indicators measured on a standardized **1-5 scale**:

### 1. Leadership and Governance
Executive sponsorship, strategic alignment, and governance maturity that ensure sustained commitment and effective decision-making throughout modernization initiatives.

**Key Indicators:**
- Executive sponsorship and active engagement
- IT strategic alignment with department mission and goals
- Governance and oversight structures with clear authority

### 2. Data and Technology Infrastructure
The foundation of technical capability, including cloud adoption, system interoperability, and data governance practices that enable successful modernization.

**Key Indicators:**
- Cloud adoption and legacy system modernization strategy
- Interoperability and API readiness for system integration
- Data strategy and governance framework

### 3. Organizational Culture and Workforce Capability
The human elements of readiness, including openness to change, digital literacy, and the presence of modern technology roles necessary to sustain innovation.

**Key Indicators:**
- Openness to change and cross-functional collaboration
- Digital literacy and skills across the organization
- Modern technology roles and continuous learning culture

### 4. Cybersecurity and Risk Management
Compliance with state mandates and the presence of proactive security governance to protect investments and public data.

**Key Indicators:**
- State policy compliance (SAM/SIMM 5300)
- Active security governance and leadership engagement

### 5. Service Delivery and Citizen Experience
The maturity of user-centered design practices and digital service offerings that directly impact California residents.

**Key Indicators:**
- Digital service adoption and maturity
- User-centered design practices and accessibility compliance

### 6. Funding, Planning, and Portfolio Management
Project management discipline, portfolio prioritization processes, and the existence of "Stage 1-ready" project documentation required for CDT approval.

**Key Indicators:**
- Project management maturity and methodology
- Portfolio prioritization processes
- Stage 1-ready project documentation and pipeline

## Expected Outcomes and Benefits

By implementing this standardized readiness assessment, California will achieve measurable improvements in modernization effectiveness:

### Objective Prioritization
Replace subjective or political decision-making with transparent, criteria-based evaluation of which departments can immediately execute modernization projects successfully.

**Benefits:**
- Consistent evaluation standards across all 170 departments
- Transparent scoring methodology that can be validated and audited
- Defensible rationale for funding allocation decisions
- Reduced perception of favoritism or political influence

### Risk Mitigation
Identify and address capability gaps before funding is allocated, reducing the likelihood of project delays, cost overruns, or failures that have plagued past IT initiatives.

**Benefits:**
- Early identification of organizational and technical obstacles
- Proactive intervention to address critical gaps
- Reduced likelihood of project failure or abandonment
- Lower overall cost of modernization through better project selection

### Accelerated Delivery
Direct funding to departments that can begin work immediately with existing governance, technical, and workforce capabilities, delivering faster value to residents.

**Benefits:**
- Reduced time from funding approval to project initiation
- Faster realization of modernization benefits
- Demonstrated early wins that build momentum and support
- More efficient use of limited funding resources

### Continuous Improvement
Establish a baseline assessment that can be repeated over time, allowing departments to track their progress and make targeted investments in capability building.

**Benefits:**
- Clear roadmap for departments to improve readiness
- Measurable progress tracking through periodic reassessment
- Identification of effective improvement strategies
- Statewide learning and best practice sharing

### Strategic Alignment
Ensure modernization investments support statewide goals and are executed by departments with the leadership commitment and strategic planning necessary for long-term success.

**Benefits:**
- Technology investments directly linked to strategic objectives
- Executive commitment to sustaining modernization beyond project completion
- Integration of technology planning with business planning
- Long-term value realization and sustained benefits

# Part 2: Conceptual Foundations

> **Section Summary:** This section describes the research basis, design principles, and domain-specific rationale behind the assessment scale.

## Background

### Strategic Context

California state government is undertaking an ambitious multi-year technology modernization effort, accelerating digital transformation to enhance public services, improve operational efficiency, strengthen cybersecurity, and respond to evolving resident needs.

Achieving these goals depends not merely on the availability of funding, but on the capacity and readiness of departments to execute transformational projects successfully. Past modernization efforts have demonstrated that success is heavily influenced by a department's:

- **Internal leadership commitment** and sustained executive engagement
- **Governance structures** that enable timely decisions and accountability
- **Technical and data infrastructure** that provides a foundation for innovation
- **Workforce capability** and openness to change
- **Cybersecurity posture** that protects investments and public trust
- **Operational maturity** in project management and service delivery

Without organizational readiness across these dimensions, even well-funded projects can face delays, cost overruns, diminished benefits, or outright failure.

### Lessons from Past Modernization Efforts

California and other large government entities have learned valuable lessons from decades of technology modernization initiatives:

**Leadership matters more than technology:** Projects with active executive sponsorship and governance succeed at dramatically higher rates than those driven solely by IT departments, regardless of the technology choices made.

**Technical debt compounds over time:** Departments that defer infrastructure modernization and data governance face exponentially increasing costs and complexity when modernization becomes unavoidable.

**Culture eats strategy for breakfast:** Organizations with change-resistant cultures and outdated workforce skills struggle to adopt even the best-designed modern systems, leading to low user adoption and failed benefits realization.

**Security cannot be bolted on:** Cybersecurity compliance and risk management must be foundational, not afterthoughts. Projects that attempt to add security late face rework, delays, and residual vulnerabilities.

**User-centered design is not optional:** Systems designed around internal processes rather than user needs deliver poor citizen experiences, lower adoption, and wasted public resources.

**Project management discipline predicts outcomes:** Departments with mature project management practices deliver on time and on budget; those without formal methodologies consistently experience overruns and scope creep.

### National and Global Best Practices

The California Technology Readiness Assessment scale and framework are informed by leading national and international models and standards:

**California State Standards and Frameworks:**
- Statewide Information Management Manual (SIMM) - security and IT governance standards
- Project Approval Lifecycle (PAL) - project management and approval framework
- State Digital Strategy - vision for inclusive, secure, and effective digital government

**Federal Technology Standards:**
- Technology Readiness Assessment (TRA) and Technology Readiness Level (TRL) scales (U.S. Department of Defense, NASA)
- Digital Services Playbook (U.S. Digital Service)
- Federal Information Technology Acquisition Reform Act (FITARA) implementation guidance

**International Digital Government Maturity Models:**
- OECD Digital Government Maturity Framework
- United Nations E-Government Development Index
- World Bank Digital Government Readiness Assessment

**Industry Frameworks and Standards:**
- Capability Maturity Model Integration (CMMI) for organizational process maturity
- IT Infrastructure Library (ITIL) for service management
- NIST Cybersecurity Framework for security program maturity
- Agile Maturity Model for modern software delivery
- Change Management Body of Knowledge (APMG, Prosci) for organizational change readiness

## Purpose and Rationale

### Why Detailed, Standardized Evaluation?

A simple checklist or binary yes/no approach fails to capture the complexity and interdependence of the factors driving successful modernization. Technology readiness exists on a continuum, with organizations at various stages of maturity across different capability areas.

The standardized **1-5 maturity scale** across each key domain enables:

**Objective Measurement**
- Consistent, repeatable scoring regardless of department size, mission, or complexity
- Elimination of subjective bias through clear definitions and examples
- Comparability across departments with different contexts and challenges

**Transparency**
- Clear rubrics and detailed examples remove ambiguity from the assessment process
- Stakeholders understand exactly what each score means
- Departments can self-assess with confidence and accuracy

**Comparability**
- Benchmarking across all 170 departments to identify patterns and outliers
- Peer group analysis (by size, mission, budget) for contextual comparison
- Longitudinal tracking over time to measure improvement

**Actionability**
- Results pinpoint specific gaps with concrete descriptions of what improvement looks like
- Scoring rubrics provide roadmap from current state to higher maturity levels
- Departments receive targeted, specific recommendations rather than vague feedback

**Defensible Funding Allocation**
- Provides clear, traceable rationale for modernization funding priorities
- Transparent criteria that can be reviewed and validated
- Reduces perception of political influence or favoritism
- Withstands scrutiny from oversight bodies and stakeholders

### Domain Design Principles

The assessment evaluates **six domains** that reflect the critical elements required for successful modernization. These domains were selected based on:

- **Research evidence** from academic studies of IT project success factors
- **Empirical evidence** from past California and federal government technology initiatives
- **Expert consensus** from practitioners, consultants, and oversight bodies
- **Strategic alignment** with California's technology goals and priorities

Each domain contains **2-3 specific indicators** that are:

- **Observable and measurable** - Can be assessed through documentation, interviews, or direct observation
- **Actionable** - Can be improved through targeted interventions
- **Relevant** - Directly impacts modernization success
- **Distinct** - Captures a unique aspect of readiness without redundancy

### Foundation for Continuous Improvement

The assessment scale serves multiple purposes beyond initial funding allocation:

**Baseline Establishment:** Creates a standardized baseline of current capabilities across all departments, enabling tracking of improvement over time.

**Gap Identification:** Clearly identifies specific areas where departments need to build capability, with concrete examples of what improvement looks like.

**Roadmap Development:** Scoring rubrics provide a natural progression path from lower to higher maturity levels, guiding improvement planning.

**Peer Learning:** Comparative results enable identification of high-performing departments whose practices can be studied and shared.

**Progress Tracking:** Annual or biennial reassessment measures improvement and validates the effectiveness of capability-building investments.

# Part 3: Implementation Procedures

> **Section Summary:** This section details the procedures for conducting the assessment across all 170 state departments.

## Assessment Objectives

- Establish a standardized baseline of organizational readiness across all state departments
- Identify departments best positioned to execute modernization projects now
- Surface gaps for targeted capacity investments
- Create transparent, defensible criteria for funding allocation
- Enable continuous improvement through reassessment

## Key Principles

- **Objectivity**: Use of standardized scoring criteria across all domains
- **Transparency**: Clearly defined scales and rating examples
- **Actionability**: Results provide specific actionable guidance
- **Comprehensiveness**: Evaluates all dimensions of readiness
- **Practicality**: Designed for departments of all sizes and structures

## Assessment Preparation

### Assessment Governance

Form a cross-functional steering committee within CDT or the oversight body consisting of:
- CDT executive sponsor (Chair)
- IT project oversight representative
- Cybersecurity/information security specialist
- Change management expert
- Financial/budget analyst
- Strategic planning representative

**Responsibilities:**
- Finalize the timeline and plan
- Guide and interpret assessments
- Validate results
- Recommend funding allocations

### Participant Selection

Primary assessment team (per department):
- CIO or IT Director
- CFO or Budget Officer
- CISO or ISO
- HR/Workforce lead
- PMO Director
- Strategic Planning Lead or Chief of Staff

**Executive Reviewer:** Department Director or Agency Head

### Preparing Materials

**Required:**
- Full assessment instrument (all domains and indicators)
- Detailed scoring rubrics (1-5 scale examples)
- Evidence template (recommended)
- Assessment submission portal (or email)
- Training webinar/orientation slide deck

**Communication Package:**
- Executive memo (Governor's Office/CDT Director)
- FAQ for common issues
- Timeline & clear milestones
- Technical assistance contacts

### Conducting Orientation & Training

**Webinar (60–90 min):**
- Overview and context
- Walkthrough of all domains and indicators
- Scoring scales explained (with examples)
- Evidence/documentation expectations
- Live Q&A; submission instructions

**Support:**
- Weekly office hours
- Dedicated email support
- Resource portal with FAQs & sample evidence

## Step-by-Step Assessment Procedures

### Phase 1: Internal Team Formation (Week 1)
- Assemble assessment team
- Kickoff meeting, assign domains, set internal schedule
- Review instrument & attend orientation
- Gather documentation

### Phase 2: Domain Assessment (Weeks 2–4)
- Each lead completes their domain section using 1-5 scales/examples
- Team reviews ratings & evidence
- Cross-checks for scoring consistency
- Present draft results for executive input
- Finalize ratings by consensus and with leadership

### Phase 3: Submission & Validation (Week 5)
- Complete and compile final form and supporting documentation
- Executive sign-off with transmittal memo
- Submit via portal or email
- Respond to follow-up/clarification requests

### Phase 4: Results & Planning (Week 6+)
- Departments receive results, peer and statewide analyses
- High-readiness: Begin project proposals for funding
- Medium- and lower-readiness: Draft and submit improvement plans
- Begin tracking of continuous improvement cycle

## Scoring and Evaluation Guidelines

### Indicator Scoring

Each indicator is rated 1-5 (see scoring rubrics/examples):
- **1:** Initial/ad-hoc (little/no formal capability)
- **2:** Emerging (some initial, sporadic efforts)
- **3:** Developing (formal practices emerging, inconsistent)
- **4:** Managed (well-documented, consistent)
- **5:** Optimized (mature, best-in-class, continuous improvement)

Guidelines:
- Score honestly: choose lower score if uncertain
- Document rationale, especially for 1 or 5
- Use evidence (audits, project records, documentation)

### Domain Scoring
- Average indicator scores within a domain (rounded to 1 decimal place)
- Apply indicator-specific weights if instructed

### Overall Readiness Score

Default weights:
- Leadership & Governance: 20%
- Data/Technology Infrastructure: 20%
- Culture & Workforce: 15%
- Cybersecurity & Risk: 15%
- Service/Citizen Experience: 15%
- Planning/Portfolio Management: 15%

**Calculated as:** (Domain Score × Weight) summed, out of 5.0

### Tier Classification
- **Tier 1 (Score 4.0–5.0):** Ready Now – Fundable
- **Tier 2 (3.0–3.9):** Near-Ready – Fund with targeted support
- **Tier 3 (2.0–2.9):** Developing – Focus on gap closure
- **Tier 4 (1.0–1.9):** Early Stage – Foundational capacity building

## Results Analysis and Interpretation

### Statewide Results
- Aggregate and analyze all results: mean, median, standard deviation, outliers
- Segment by size, mission, or budget for benchmarking
- Identify domain-level strengths/weaknesses statewide

### Department Results Interpretation
- Analyze strengths (high domain scores)
- Analyze gaps (lowest scores, root causes)
- Assess readiness balance and "fatal flaws"
- Set improvement priorities and estimate investment/timelines required

## Funding Allocation Recommendations

### Tier 1 (4.0–5.0)
- Immediate funding through Tech Modernization Fund
- Fast-track approval/minor oversight
- Fund large, transformative projects upon strong proposal

### Tier 2 (3.0–3.9)
- Conditional/project funding with required improvement plan for gaps
- Enhanced CDT oversight/technical assistance
- Stage in large investments as capacity builds

### Tier 3 (2.0–2.9)
- Invest in organizational capability building, not modernization projects
- Provide technical assistance, training, mentoring
- 12–18 month gap-closure roadmap before major projects

### Tier 4 (1.0–1.9)
- No project funding; foundational organizational development only
- May require external or embedded improvement support
- 24–36 month transformation roadmap

## Capability Development Planning

**For Tiers 2–4:**
- Prioritize 2–3 vital improvements for initial focus
- Set specific and measurable goals and timelines
- Identify support/resources: budget, assistance, training, leadership commitment
- Assign accountable executive sponsor
- Set milestones, schedule reassessment (quarterly/annually)

### Statewide Capability Initiatives
- Develop standard training/workshops (governance, cloud, project management, UX, change management)
- Encourage peer networks (CIO, security, project management)
- Offer technical assistance and best-practice templates

### Continuous Improvement Cycle
- Reassess readiness annually
- Analyze trends, publish best practices and case studies
- Adjust funding/support strategies according to improvement

## Implementation Timeline

| Phase | Weeks | Activities |
|-------|-------|------------|
| **Preparation** | 1–2 | Finalize instruments/materials, steering and communication plans; open portal, schedule training |
| **Launch/Orientation** | 3 | Issue launch memo, deliver orientation |
| **Assessment** | 4–7 | Complete/submit assessments, get support if needed |
| **Analysis/Validation** | 8–9 | Validate results, interview select departments |
| **Results Out** | 10 | Distribute results/analysis statewide/private |
| **Planning** | 11–12 | Project proposals/improvement plans by readiness tier, finalize funding support |
| **Ongoing** | — | Quarterly progress review; annual reassessment; continuous learning |
